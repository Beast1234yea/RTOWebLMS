## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Skill Set Overview

**This training course is based on the nationally recognised skill set RIISS00054 - Traffic Controller Skill Set.**

**Source:** Australian Government. (2025). RIISS00054 Traffic Controller Skill Set. training.gov.au. Retrieved November 2025, from https://training.gov.au/Training/Details/RIISS00054

**Skill Set Application:**

> "This skill set covers the skills and knowledge required for persons working as a traffic controller at worksites, including road construction and maintenance."

**Source:** RIISS00054 Skill Set Descriptor, training.gov.au

**This skill set comprises four units:**
1. **RIICOM201E** - Communicate in the workplace
2. **RIIWHS201E** - Work safely and follow WHS policies and procedures
3. **RIIWHS205E** - Control traffic with stop-slow bat
4. **RIIWHS206** - Control traffic with portable traffic control devices and temporary traffic signs

---

### 1.2 Traffic Controller Role

Traffic controllers play a critical role in protecting workers, road users, and the public at road worksites.

**Key Responsibilities:**
- Control traffic flow using stop-slow bats and other traffic control devices
- Implement traffic management plans
- Communicate effectively with workers and road users
- Respond to changing conditions and emergencies
- Follow WHS procedures to protect themselves and others

**Legal Framework:**

Under Model WHS legislation, persons conducting a business or undertaking (PCBUs) must ensure that road workers and road users are protected from risks associated with traffic at worksites.

**Source:** Safe Work Australia. (2024). Model Work Health and Safety Regulations, Part 2 (Duties). Retrieved November 2025, from https://www.safeworkaustralia.gov.au/law-and-regulation/model-whs-laws

### 2.1 Communication Requirements

**Unit:** RIICOM201E - Communicate in the workplace

**Source:** Australian Government. (2025). RIICOM201E Communicate in the workplace. training.gov.au. Retrieved November 2025, from https://training.gov.au/Training/Details/RIICOM201E

Effective communication is essential for traffic controllers to:
- Understand work requirements and traffic management plans
- Coordinate with other workers
- Communicate with road users
- Report hazards and incidents
- Use communication equipment

### 2.2 Communication Methods

Traffic controllers must be competent in various communication methods:

**Face-to-Face Communication:**
- Clear verbal instructions
- Active listening
- Confirming understanding

**Hand Signals:**
- Standard traffic control hand signals
- Visibility and clarity
- Coordination between controllers

**Communication Equipment:**
- Two-way radios (UHF/VHF)
- Mobile phones
- Emergency communication systems

**Written Communication:**
- Completing site documentation
- Incident reports
- Logbooks and checklists

**Source:** RIICOM201E Performance Criteria, training.gov.au

---

### 2.3 Traffic Management Plans (TMPs)

Traffic controllers must be able to interpret and follow Traffic Management Plans.

**TMPs typically include:**
- Site layout and traffic flow diagrams
- Traffic control device placement
- Signage requirements
- Speed limits
- Worker positions and responsibilities
- Communication protocols
- Emergency procedures

**Authority:** Traffic Management Plans must be prepared by qualified traffic management designers or in accordance with relevant road authority requirements.

**Source:** Austroads. (2023). Guide to Temporary Traffic Management. Retrieved November 2025, from https://austroads.com.au

**Workers must:**
- Follow workplace health and safety procedures
- Use PPE correctly
- Report hazards and incidents
- Not willfully interfere with safety equipment
- Cooperate with safety requirements

---

### 3.2 Hazard Identification and Risk Control

**Risk Management Process:**

The risk management process involves:
1. **Identify** hazards
2. **Assess** risks
3. **Control** risks
4. **Review** controls

**Source:** Safe Work Australia. (2018). Model Code of Practice: How to manage work health and safety risks (Section 3: The risk management process). Retrieved November 2025, from https://www.safeworkaustralia.gov.au/doc/model-code-practice-how-manage-work-health-and-safety-risks

**Common Hazards for Traffic Controllers:**
- Being struck by vehicles
- Pedestrian-vehicle interactions
- Heat stress and sun exposure
- Fatigue from long shifts
- Aggressive drivers
- Poor visibility conditions
- Noise exposure
- Manual handling

---

### 3.3 Hierarchy of Control

Risk controls must be implemented following the hierarchy of control:

| Level | Control Type | Example for Traffic Control |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **1. Elimination** | Remove the hazard | Plan work for low traffic times; close road completely |
| **2. Substitution** | Replace with safer alternative | Use automated systems or lower-risk methods |
| **3. Isolation** | Separate workers from hazard | Install physical barriers; increase separation distance |
| **4. Engineering Controls** | Use equipment to reduce risk | Positive protection vehicles; electronic signs |
| **5. Administrative Controls** | Procedures and training | Traffic management plans; shift rotations; training |
| **6. PPE** | Personal protective equipment | High-visibility clothing; safety footwear; hard hats |

**Source:** Safe Work Australia. (2018). Model Code of Practice: How to manage work health and safety risks (Section 3.3: Controlling risks). Retrieved November 2025, from https://www.safeworkaustralia.gov.au/doc/model-code-practice-how-manage-work-health-and-safety-risks

---

### 3.4 Personal Protective Equipment

**PPE Requirements for Traffic Controllers:**

Traffic controllers must wear appropriate PPE including:
- **High-visibility clothing** (meeting AS/NZS 4602 or AS/NZS 1906.4)
  - Day use: fluorescent yellow-green or orange-red
  - Night use: retroreflective material
- **Safety footwear** (steel cap boots)
- **Sun protection** (hat, sunscreen, sunglasses)
- **Hearing protection** (if required by noise assessment)
- **Hard hat** (if overhead hazards present)

**High-Visibility Clothing Standard:**

> "Workers exposed to traffic must wear high-visibility safety garments complying with AS/NZS 4602.1 or AS/NZS 1906.4."

**Source:** State road authority requirements (varies by jurisdiction - check local requirements)

## 4. TRAFFIC CONTROL WITH STOP-SLOW BAT

### 4.1 Unit Overview

**Unit:** RIIWHS205E - Control traffic with stop-slow bat

**Source:** Australian Government. (2025). RIIWHS205E Control traffic with stop-slow bat. training.gov.au. Retrieved November 2025, from https://training.gov.au/Training/Details/RIIWHS205E

**Unit Application:**

> "This unit of competency specifies the outcomes required to control traffic with a stop-slow bat at worksites."

**Source:** RIIWHS205E Unit Descriptor, training.gov.au

---

### 4.2 Stop-Slow Bat Specifications

**Standard Stop-Slow Bat:**
- **Diameter:** 450mm (±10mm)
- **Shape:** Octagonal (STOP side), Circular (SLOW side)
- **Colors:**
  - STOP side: Red background with white border and white lettering
  - SLOW side: Yellow background with black border and black lettering
- **Material:** Lightweight, durable, retroreflective
- **Handle:** Minimum 600mm long

**Compliance:**

Traffic control devices must comply with specifications in the relevant jurisdiction's traffic control manual (e.g., NSW Traffic Control at Worksites Manual, Queensland MUTCD).

**Source:** State road authority specifications (varies by jurisdiction)

---

### 4.3 Bat Signals and Techniques

**STOP Signal:**
- Hold bat with STOP side facing traffic
- Extend arm straight out from body
- Hold bat steady and visible
- Maintain eye contact with driver
- Use free hand to reinforce signal if needed

**SLOW Signal:**
- Hold bat with SLOW side facing traffic
- Rotate bat slowly back and forth in a sweeping motion
- Maintain eye contact with approaching vehicles
- Be prepared to change to STOP if required

**Positioning:**
- Stand in a visible position on the shoulder or behind barriers
- Never stand in the traffic lane
- Position to allow escape route if vehicle does not stop
- Ensure good visibility in both directions

**Communication Between Controllers:**
- Use two-way radios for coordination
- Confirm traffic releases
- Warn of approaching heavy vehicles or hazards
- Regular check-ins

---

### 4.4 Setting Up Traffic Control

**Pre-Start Checks:**
1. Receive and understand the Traffic Management Plan
2. Inspect stop-slow bat for damage
3. Check PPE is clean and in good condition
4. Test communication equipment
5. Conduct site induction and hazard identification
6. Confirm positions with other controllers
7. Verify signage and device placement

**Worksite Setup:**
- Install advance warning signs first (working from outside in)
- Set up traffic control devices according to TMP
- Position delineation and barriers
- Establish safe refuge areas
- Ensure clear sightlines
- Check visibility of signs and controllers

**Source:** RIIWHS205E Performance Criteria and applicable state traffic management manuals

---

### 4.5 Traffic Control Procedures

**Standard Procedures:**

1. **Stop Traffic:**
   - Display STOP signal clearly
   - Wait for all vehicles to stop before releasing other direction
   - Communicate with other controller to release traffic

2. **Release Traffic:**
   - Confirm roadway is clear
   - Change bat to SLOW position
   - Wave traffic through using approved signals
   - Monitor traffic flow

3. **Emergency Vehicles:**
   - Priority passage for emergency vehicles (ambulance, fire, police)
   - Clear traffic from both directions
   - Signal emergency vehicle through
   - Resume normal operations

4. **Heavy Vehicles:**
   - Allow extra time and distance
   - Consider road width and turning requirements
   - Communicate with driver if necessary
   - Watch for trailer swing

### 5.1 Unit Overview

**Unit:** RIIWHS206 - Control traffic with portable traffic control devices and temporary traffic signs

**Source:** Australian Government. (2025). RIIWHS206 Control traffic with portable traffic control devices and temporary traffic signs. training.gov.au. Retrieved November 2025, from https://training.gov.au/Training/Details/RIIWHS206

**Unit Application:**

> "This unit of competency specifies the outcomes required to control traffic using portable traffic control devices and temporary traffic signs."

**Source:** RIIWHS206 Unit Descriptor, training.gov.au

---

### 5.2 Types of Traffic Control Devices

**Warning Signs:**
- Roadwork ahead signs
- Speed limit signs
- Lane closure signs
- Hazard warning signs

**Direction Signs:**
- Lane diversion arrows
- Detour signs
- Directional chevrons

**Delineation Devices:**
- Traffic cones
- Portable barriers
- Delineator posts
- Temporary edge lines

**Illumination:**
- Flashing lights
- Illuminated signs
- Floodlights for night work

**Protection Devices:**
- Truck-mounted attenuators (TMAs)
- Portable barriers
- Water-filled barriers

**Compliance:**

All traffic control devices must comply with:
- Manual of Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) for the relevant state/territory
- AS 1742 series (Manual of Uniform Traffic Control Devices)
- Road authority specifications

**Source:** State/territory road authority requirements

---

### 5.3 Sign Placement and Spacing

**Advance Warning Distance:**

Signs must be placed at specified distances before the worksite to give drivers adequate warning time.

**Typical advance warning distances (vary by speed zone and conditions):**
- **60 km/h zone:** 50-90m
- **80 km/h zone:** 90-135m
- **100 km/h zone:** 150-200m
- **110 km/h zone:** 200-250m

**Multiple signs:** Space at 50% of advance warning distance

**Source:** State traffic control manuals (e.g., NSW Traffic Control at Worksites Manual, Queensland MUTCD)

**Installation Principles:**
- Install in order (advance warning first)
- Ensure stability and wind resistance
- Check visibility and angle
- Use ballast or weights
- Position clear of traffic
- Regular inspection and maintenance

---

### 5.4 Lane Closures and Tapers

**Taper Design:**

Tapers guide traffic from one lane into another. Taper length depends on:
- Operating speed
- Number of lanes being merged
- Available space

**Taper Length Formula (typical):**
- Taper length (meters) = Operating speed (km/h) × multiplier
- Multiplier varies by jurisdiction and conditions

**Taper Types:**
- **Merging taper:** Transitions traffic from multiple lanes to fewer lanes
- **Diverging taper:** Splits traffic into additional lanes
- **Two-way taper:** Provides transition for two-way traffic on one lane

**Delineation:**
- Use traffic cones or delineators spaced at regular intervals
- Closer spacing at the start of taper
- Ensure continuous visual guidance

## 6. EMERGENCY PROCEDURES

### 6.1 Emergency Response

**Types of Emergencies:**
- Traffic incidents within worksite
- Worker injury
- Vehicle breakdown blocking traffic
- Fire or hazardous materials spill
- Severe weather
- Aggressive or impaired drivers

**Immediate Actions:**
1. **Ensure personal safety first**
2. **Stop traffic** if safe to do so
3. **Call emergency services** (000)
4. **Notify supervisor** and other workers
5. **Implement emergency traffic control** as per TMP
6. **Provide assistance** if trained and safe to do so
7. **Document incident** after emergency is resolved

**Source:** RIIWHS205E and RIIWHS206 Performance Criteria

---

### 6.2 Incident Notification

**Notifiable Incidents:**

Under Model WHS laws, certain incidents must be reported to the work health and safety regulator.

**Notifiable incidents include:**
- Death of a person
- Serious injury or illness
- Dangerous incidents

**Notification must be made immediately by phone, then in writing within 48 hours.**

**Source:** Safe Work Australia. (2024). Model Work Health and Safety Regulations, Part 3.3 (Incident notification). Retrieved November 2025, from https://www.safeworkaustralia.gov.au/law-and-regulation/model-whs-laws

**Workplace Reporting:**
- Complete incident report forms
- Photograph scene if safe (for investigation)
- Preserve evidence
- Cooperate with investigations
- Participate in debrief and review

---

### 6.3 Fatigue Management

**Fatigue Risks:**

Traffic controlling is demanding work. Fatigue can impair:
- Reaction time
- Decision making
- Concentration
- Awareness of hazards

**Fatigue Controls:**
- Limit shift length and consecutive days worked
- Provide adequate breaks and rest periods
- Rotate tasks
- Ensure adequate hydration and nutrition
- Provide shade and rest areas
- Monitor weather conditions
- Report fatigue to supervisor

**Source:** Safe Work Australia. (2018). Model Code of Practice: How to manage work health and safety risks (Section 4: Specific hazards). Retrieved November 2025, from https://www.safeworkaustralia.gov.au/doc/model-code-practice-how-manage-work-health-and-safety-risks
